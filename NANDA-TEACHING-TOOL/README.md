It includes the initial clinical case input, the structured educational output generated by the AI multi-agent system, and an expert analysis validating the clinical accuracy of the generated content.

## Purpose of the Flow
The **NANDA-TEACHING-TOOL-SQL** is a multi-agent Langflow application designed to act as an advanced educational aid for healthcare workers and nursing students. It transforms a standard deep-research AI pipeline into a specialized clinical reasoning mentor.

The core objective is to teach the **Relationship Between the NANDA Database and Clinical Reasoning**. By integrating an SQL database containing the official NANDA-I taxonomy (along with NIC and NOC linkages) directly into the agent workflow, this tool grounds the AI's reasoning in factual, standardized medical terminology. This enforces "truth-finding" and prevents the LLMs from hallucinating nursing diagnoses.

### Key Educational Pillars Addressed:
1. **Standardized Language for Structured Thinking:** Translating raw patient data into precise NANDA vocabulary.
2. **Integration with the Nursing Process:** Guiding the user from Assessment to Diagnosis and Planning.
3. **Evidence-Based Practice (EBP):** Linking decisions to validated interventions (NIC) and measurable outcomes (NOC).
4. **Promotion of Critical Thinking:** Forcing the evaluation of multiple data sources to justify clinical choices.

## Flow Architecture
This pipeline utilizes five distinct AI agents powered by SURF's WILLMA endpoints:
* **Agent 1 (Clinical Reasoning Assistant):** Breaks down the user's clinical case. It uses the **SQL Database Tool** to query the internal NANDA taxonomy for matching defining characteristics and related factors.
* **Agent 2 (Summarization Expert):** Distills the gathered database and web search evidence, organizing it logically into the nursing process (Assessment → Diagnosis → Planning).
* **Agents 3 & 4 (Reviewers / Nursing Educators):** Validate the proposed diagnoses. They explicitly cross-reference the suggested labels with the SQL database to ensure the patient's objective/subjective data truly meets the formal NANDA criteria.
* **Agent 5 (Professional Nursing Educator):** Synthesizes the validated data into a structured, educational feedback report designed to teach the user *why* a specific diagnosis is correct.

## How to Use

1. **Import the Flow:** Upload `NANDA-TEACHING-TOOL-SQL-FIXED.json` into your Langflow environment (v1.10.1+).
2. **Configure the SQL Database:** Locate the `SQL Database` component. Enter your valid Database URI that points to your structured NANDA-I/NIC/NOC database (e.g., a MySQL or PostgreSQL connection string).
3. **Configure WILLMA API Keys:** Ensure all five `Agent (WILLMA SURF)` nodes have your SURF API key and the correct Base URL (`https://chat-ai.academiccloud.nl/v1`).
4. **Run the Chat:** Open the Langflow chat interface and input a clinical case study.

## 1. The Clinical Scenario (Input Query)

**Clinical Case Study:**
A 68-year-old male patient is admitted to the ward with an exacerbation of Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD).

**Assessment Data:**
- Respiratory rate: 28 breaths per minute
- Oxygen saturation (SpO2): 88% on room air
- Observation: Patient is sitting in a tripod position and using accessory muscles to breathe.
- Subjective: Patient states, "I feel like I can't catch my breath" and appears visibly anxious and restless.

**Task:**
Analyze this assessment data to determine the most accurate primary NANDA-I diagnosis. Walk me through the clinical reasoning process, including the specific defining characteristics that justify this diagnosis, and suggest linked NIC interventions and NOC outcomes.

## 2. The Agent's Output (Structured Teaching Report)

### Standardized Language for Structured Thinking

| Element | Standardized Term | Why it matters in this case |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Problem label** | **Impaired Gas Exchange (NANDA‑I 0010)** | Directly captures the objective finding of **low SpO₂ (88 % on room air)** together with the patient’s dyspnea, tachypnea, and use of accessory muscles. |
| **Related Factor (RF)** | Airway obstruction / V‑Q mismatch secondary to **COPD exacerbation** | The RF explains *why* the gas exchange is compromised and guides the choice of interventions (bronchodilators, positioning, low‑flow O₂). |
| **Defining Characteristics (DCs)** | • Dyspnea (subjective “I can’t catch my breath”) <br>• Tachypnea (RR 28 bpm) <br>• Decreased SpO₂ (88 % on RA) <br>• Use of accessory muscles & tripod position <br>• Restlessness / anxiety | Each DC is documented in the assessment and is listed in the NANDA‑I taxonomy for **Impaired Gas Exchange**. |
| **NIC (Nursing Interventions Classification)** | Airway Management (4350), Oxygen Therapy (3320), Positioning (0840), Respiratory Monitoring (3320‑02), Anxiety Reduction (5270), Patient Education – Disease Process (5600) | Using NIC codes guarantees that the plan is **communicable**, **searchable**, and **compatible** with electronic health‑record decision‑support tools. |
| **NOC (Nursing Outcomes Classification)** | Gas Exchange (0404), Respiratory Status: Ventilation (0402), Anxiety Level (1402), Activity Tolerance (1303) | NOC outcomes provide measurable end‑points (e.g., SpO₂ ≥ 92 %, RR 12‑20 bpm, VAS anxiety ≤ 3/10). |

### Integration with the Nursing Process (Assessment → Diagnosis → Planning)

#### A. Assessment
| Objective data | Subjective data | Clinical significance |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| RR = 28 bpm (tachypnea) | “I feel like I can’t catch my breath.” | ↑ work of breathing → impaired ventilation. |
| SpO₂ = 88 % on room air (hypoxemia) | Appears anxious & restless. | ↓ oxygen delivery to tissues; anxiety raises metabolic demand. |
| Tripod position, accessory‑muscle use | – | Classic sign of **airway obstruction** and increased inspiratory effort. |

> **Critical‑thinking tip:** When an **objective gas‑exchange abnormality** (SpO₂ < 90 % or ABG abnormality) is present, the NANDA‑I hierarchy dictates that **Impaired Gas Exchange** outranks pattern‑related diagnoses (e.g., *Ineffective Breathing Pattern*).

#### B. Diagnosis (Primary)
**Impaired Gas Exchange (0010)**
- **Defining Characteristics present:** Dyspnea, Tachypnea, Decreased SpO₂, Accessory-muscle use, Restlessness.
- **Related Factor:** COPD exacerbation → airway obstruction & V/Q mismatch.

#### C. Planning (NIC → NOC)
* **Oxygen Therapy (3320):** Initiate low‑flow O₂ (2 L/min via nasal cannula) to correct the **decreased SpO₂** DC. Low‑flow is essential in COPD to avoid CO₂ retention.
* **Positioning (0840):** Place patient in semi‑Fowler’s or maintain tripod as tolerated to improve diaphragmatic excursion.
* **Respiratory Monitoring (3320‑02):** Continuous pulse‑ox, RR, HR, BP. Obtain ABG within the first hour.
* **Target Outcome (NOC - Gas Exchange 0404):** SpO₂ ≥ 92 % on prescribed O₂; PaO₂ > 60 mm Hg.

### Evidence‑Based Practice (EBP)
| Evidence source | Key finding relevant to this case | How it informs the plan |
|-----------------|-----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **GOLD 2023 COPD Guidelines** | Low‑flow O₂ (≤ 2 L/min) is recommended for COPD exacerbations to avoid CO₂ retention. | Guides the **Oxygen Therapy** titration target and flow rate. |
| **NANDA‑I 2023 Taxonomy** | “Impaired Gas Exchange” lists the exact DCs observed. | Confirms the diagnosis selection. |

### Decision Support & Patient Outcomes
**Clinical Decision Support (CDS) Trigger:**
- **If SpO₂ < 90 % on room air** → automatic alert to initiate **Oxygen Therapy (3320)** and **Respiratory Monitoring (3320‑02)**.
- **If RR > 30 bpm OR accessory‑muscle use persists** → prompt escalation to the provider.

## 3. Expert Analysis & Validation of the Output

The output provided by the teaching tool is highly accurate, logically structured, and correctly utilizes NANDA-I, NIC, and NOC terminology to demonstrate clinical reasoning. 

### Diagnostic Accuracy and Hierarchy
**Valid and Accurate.** The choice of **Impaired Gas Exchange (00030)** as the primary diagnosis is clinically correct for a patient with an SpO₂ of 88% on room air. The distinction made in the response's "Critical-thinking tip" is particularly accurate: while the patient exhibits signs of an *Ineffective Breathing Pattern* (tachypnea, use of accessory muscles), the presence of hypoxemia indicates a failure at the alveolar-capillary membrane, making *Impaired Gas Exchange* the higher priority and more severe diagnosis. 

### Standardized Language (NANDA-I, NIC, NOC)
**Valid and Accurate.** The listed defining characteristics (dyspnea, tachypnea, decreased SpO₂, use of accessory muscles, restlessness) are all officially recognized for *Impaired Gas Exchange* in the NANDA-I taxonomy. The suggested interventions (Oxygen Therapy 3320, Airway Management 4350) and outcomes (Gas Exchange 0404) are the correct standardized linkages.

### Integration with the Nursing Process
**Valid and Accurate.** The response excellently maps the raw clinical data (subjective and objective) to the defining characteristics. This is a perfect demonstration of the assessment phase feeding into the diagnostic phase. The progression from the diagnosis to specific, actionable NICs and measurable NOCs completes the planning phase logically.

### Evidence-Based Practice and Decision Support
**Valid and Accurate.** The inclusion of external evidence (GOLD Guidelines for COPD) to justify the specific oxygen flow rate (low-flow to avoid CO₂ retention) demonstrates advanced clinical reasoning beyond just matching vocabulary. The "Decision Support" section correctly illustrates how standardized terminology integrates with EMRs to trigger alerts for patient safety.
